On Cinema — Deleuze

II. The Frame and the Shot

Reading notes, Fall 2021

Deleuze defines the set as a closed system that includes everything that appears in the image or the frame. The set is a collection of data or images. The frame can be understood as one's perception or the camera's depth of field. The depth of field determines the amount of content within the frame. When there are data in the field, there are always elements out of field. "The out of field refers to what is neither seen nor understood, but is nevertheless perfectly present." (Deleuze, p. 16) The concept of framing can refer back to Bogue's reading, where he uses body perceptions, such as olfactory and touch, to define elements like scents or temperature. The out of field can be seen as elements or contents that the body or the camera has not yet included. The contents rely on the set; meanwhile, the set is related to a more extensive web of contents. In other words, a closed system is never completely closed since all systems are related to a larger system, like the concept of limit zero in calculus, where the result will be close to zero but never truly at zero. The frame isolates a section of the set. It includes and defines contents in the field and excludes anything beyond the depth of field. Within the exclusion, there is "relative out of field" and "absolute out of field." The "relatively out of field" expands the spatial quality of the frame and is often suggested in the frame, whereas the "absolute out of field" is most likely to be uncanny and unexpected.

The shot or the plane is the movement image. In short, it is an extension of an immobile section. A shot captures or abstracts movements. The movement itself can be seen as a set, but the shot only records the specific duration where the movement happens. "The shot, that is to say, consciousness traces a movement which means the things between which it arises are continuously reuniting, and the whole is continuously dividing between the things." (Deleuze, p. 20) The common trait both shot and immobile sections share is that they are both dependent on concrete duration. If movement or the process of metamorphosis is dependent on change, then the shot is dependent on the concrete duration where the specific section of the process is happening. The shot also includes multiple movements. It is a section of the set. "The plan, is a 'unity of movement'… plan as spatial distance and temporal continuity." (Bogue, p. 43) The set contains data, which can be multiple movements happening simultaneously. The shot is an image of the set, which is an immobile section or a slice of space, as movement is defined as the continuous translation of space.

Deleuze further synthesizes the idea of images into three types: perception images, action images, and affection images. According to Bergson, all senses of memory are based on the human preestablished image or expectations of matter. Perception is a translation of processing objective data and forming subjective conclusions based on preestablished images. For example, adding sugar into a glass of water and seeing the sugar melt gives the observer the image that sugared water will be sweet. Drinking the glass of sugared water and forming the conclusion that sugared water is sweet is based on the image that sugar is sweet. Perception image is based on one's ability to connect one image to another. Action image is part of perception image but with time. After consuming the glass of sugared water, the body reacts based on perception: either spitting out the water since it is too sweet, or adding more sugar. The key is the delay in reaction, in which one receives data, forms conclusions, then moves to reaction. The affection image is defined as indifferent: "affection: consciousness replies that it is present indeed… that it fades and disappears as soon as my activity, by becoming automatic, shows that consciousness is no longer needed." (Bergson, p. 18) The affection image can be understood as a subconscious reaction when one is not consciously aware of the decision-making process. The indifference in perception defines this type of image.
